# Multiflux Theory and Paradigms in Turbulence in Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD)

## 1. Introduction

The study of turbulence remains one of the most challenging and fundamental problems in fluid dynamics and computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Traditional modeling approaches largely rely on the Navier-Stokes equations and consider turbulence as a perturbation of an underlying laminar flow state. The **Multiflux Theory** proposes a paradigm shift, suggesting that the natural state of fluid flow is neither laminar nor turbulent per se, but rather an organized superposition of multiple laminar subflows (multiflux). This report explores the conceptual foundations, historical context, and current state of the art of the Multiflux Theory and its implications for turbulence modeling and CFD.

---

## 2. Summary of Concepts and Key Arguments

### 2.1 The Multiflux Theory

- **Core Premise**: Turbulence is not a perturbation of laminar flow but arises from the nonlinear superposition of multiple local laminar subflows (subfluxes).
- **Phenomenological Evidence**: Application of KMeans clustering to turbulent flows identifies 8-12 coherent flow structures, suggesting that natural fluid behavior operates in a reduced-order regime rather than a fully chaotic state.
- **Implication**: The traditionally accepted laminar flow state is an artificial, high-energy condition, not the natural baseline state. Rest or organized multiflux represents the natural state.
- **Closure Problem**: The Multiflux Theory introduces a closure term $\vec{f}_i$ that requires rigorous validation and development, delegated to the scientific community with direct access to DNS resources and modeling expertise.

### 2.2 Paradigm Shift and Scientific Skepticism

- The theory challenges a century-old foundation of turbulence modeling, suggesting that Navier-Stokes-based laminar flow as a baseline is fundamentally unstable.
- Historical analogies highlight the resistance new paradigms face:
  - **Continental Drift (Wegener)**: Initially rejected due to unexplained mechanisms.
  - **Germ Theory (Semmelweis)**: Ridiculed despite empirical evidence.
  - **Quantum Theory (Planck)**: Initially dismissed as mathematical fiction.
- The risk lies not with the proponent but with the scientific community potentially clinging to outdated or unstable paradigms.

## 3. Evolution of the Theme

- **Traditional View**: Turbulence modeled as a perturbation on laminar flow; extensive efforts focused on closure models based on Navier-Stokes equations.
- **Emergence of Multiflux Theory**: Proposes turbulence as inherently composed of multiple interacting laminar subflows rather than a simple perturbation.
- **Computational Techniques**: Utilization of clustering algorithms (e.g., KMeans) to detect coherent structures supports the reduced-order nature of turbulence.
- **Ongoing Challenges**: Development of mathematically rigorous closure models and validation via high-fidelity DNS remains an open research area.

---

## 4. State of the Art

- **Conceptual Status**: The Multiflux Theory is currently a conceptual framework, proposing a holistic reorganization of turbulence paradigms.
- **Computational Evidence**: Data-driven techniques such as KMeans clustering reveal coherent structures aligning with Multiflux Theory predictions.
- **Modeling and Validation**: The closure term $\vec{f}_i$ introduced by the theory awaits rigorous mathematical formulation and DNS-based validation.
- **Community Involvement**: The theory calls for the CFD community’s active engagement to develop, test, and potentially adopt this paradigm, emphasizing the importance of open-minded skepticism and curiosity.
